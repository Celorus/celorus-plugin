# Celorus voice

One page. For anyone — person or agent — writing copy that carries the Celorus name.

## The stance

Write like an operator who has done the work, not a template filling slots.
Celorus sells audit-grade corporate intelligence: provenance on every claim. The
writing has to earn the same trust the product promises. Confident, plain,
specific. Nothing shouts.

## Six rules

1. **Lead with the outcome, not the plumbing.** Say what was decided — whether to
   pursue, who to engage, why now — before (or instead of) which filing or feed it
   came from. Name a source only where the source *is* the value (a provenance or
   trust claim). *Rationale: the buyer cares about the decision; the machinery is
   evidence, not the headline.*
2. **No em dashes.** Use a period, a comma, or a colon. Two sentences beat one
   dramatic break. *Rationale: em dashes read as marketing cadence; we want the
   register of a memo.*
3. **Cut hedges.** Delete "we believe," "arguably," "in many cases," "could
   potentially." State it, or don't. If a claim needs a caveat, make the caveat a
   fact with a number.
4. **Be specific.** A named number, date, entity, or mechanism beats an adjective.
   "Cuts diligence from 3 weeks to 2 days" not "dramatically faster."
5. **Vary sentence length.** Break up parallel triads and matched clauses. A short
   sentence lands. Then a longer one carries the reasoning. Rhythm signals a human
   read the draft.
6. **Claims are auditable.** Anything factual should be traceable to a source the
   reader could check. If you can't cite it, soften it to opinion or drop it.

## Register

| Trait      | We are…                          | We are not…                        |
|------------|----------------------------------|------------------------------------|
| Tone       | Institutional, calm, exact       | Breathless, hype-driven, jokey     |
| Authority  | Earned by evidence               | Asserted by adjective              |
| Person     | Direct ("you get", "Celorus does") | Corporate-passive ("it is provided") |
| Precision  | Numbers, names, dates            | "Best-in-class", "seamless", "robust" |

## Words to avoid

Revolutionary · seamless · robust · best-in-class · cutting-edge · leverage (as a
verb) · unlock · supercharge · game-changer · synergy · effortless · simply just.
They signal the template we are writing against.

## Before / after

- **Before:** "Celorus leverages cutting-edge AI to seamlessly unlock
  game-changing insights across your entire workflow."
- **After:** "Celorus reads the filing, the feed, and the CRM, and tells you which
  three accounts to call this week and why."

- **Before:** "We believe our platform could potentially help reduce research time
  quite significantly in many cases."
- **After:** "Diligence that took three weeks takes two days. Every figure links to
  its source."

## The signatures (keep these)

- Product tagline register: *"We don't sell software. We deliver AI-native
  outcomes."* Plain claim, no adornment.
- One petrol word, not italics, when a headline needs emphasis.
- Provenance line under a generated artifact: quiet, mono, factual.
